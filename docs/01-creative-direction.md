## The Thesis (locked)

**YantraCore reveals the machinery that moves modern business.**

### Philosophy (locked)

**Apps that solve real world problems — AI enabled.**

"Yantra" (यन्त्र) is Sanskrit for *instrument, mechanism, machine*. Most software studios sell outcomes ("we deliver results"). YantraCore's name already gives us something rarer to sell: **the craft of the machine itself.** The site treats software as a living mechanism — visible, beautiful, in motion — and the site itself becomes a demonstration of that thesis. Every interaction is a small proof of what we can build.

This thesis does three things at once:
1. Owns the name instead of fighting it.
2. Justifies an unconventional, animated, scroll-driven site as *not decoration* but *the actual sales argument*.
3. Lets glass, animated borders, 3D, and motion all serve one idea instead of being a feature parade.

### What the site has to carry

The thesis must hold up against what YantraCore actually does, which is three things at once:

- **Studio products** — *Restroverse* (AI-enabled hospitality platform, pre-launch) and *Jimbo* (AI agent for WhatsApp/Instagram/Facebook/SMS, deeply integrated with Restroverse). These are real, ambitious, AI-native products — the strongest evidence we can put on the site.
- **Client work** — software builds across Shopify, WordPress, custom web/app, databases, APIs, AWS, and more. Generalist studio capability.
- **Community work** — *Shramdan* (nonprofit, "works by the community for the community"). This is the soul of the company; without it, YantraCore reads as just-another-agency.

The thesis "mechanisms that move modern business" covers the products and client work cleanly. Shramdan needs to live alongside it as a counterweight — not "we also do nonprofit," but "we build mechanisms because we believe software should be in service of people." We'll feel this out in the manifesto chapter.

## Mood: Crystalline Futurism

Picture a dark workshop where the machines are made of light and glass. Not cold sci-fi. Not neon cyberpunk. Closer to a high-end watchmaker's atelier rendered in WebGL — quiet, precise, alive. Glass panels float in deep space. Borders trace themselves like circuit ink finding its path. A 3D logo turns slowly in the center, the way a mechanical movement turns under a jeweler's loupe.

**Reference temperature:**
- Dark canvas always (deep ink, not pure black)
- Color used like jewelry — sparingly, with intent
- Light treated as material — volumetric, soft, never flat
- Movement is slow and confident, never frantic
- Glass is the dominant surface language; everything else is supporting cast

## Voice

The motion and visuals carry most of the brand voice. Copy should be:
- **Short.** Headlines that breathe.
- **Quiet.** No "revolutionize," "synergy," "leverage."
- **Specific.** "We shipped Banwo's booking system in 11 weeks" beats "we deliver results."
- **A little poetic, but never precious.** Earn each metaphor.

## Motion Language

Three principles, in priority order:

1. **Slow is the new fast.** Animation durations default to 700–1200ms, not 300ms. The web is trained to flick. Award sites earn attention by being unhurried.
2. **Scroll is the protagonist.** The scroll wheel/finger becomes the user's hand on the machine. Most major reveals are scroll-driven, not autoplay.
3. **Easing tells the story.** Default ease: `cubic-bezier(0.22, 1, 0.36, 1)` — a confident exit with a gentle settle. No bounce, no linear, no rushed `ease-out`.

A working metaphor: imagine you're operating a glass-and-brass mechanism with a single dial. Everything responds to your input, but with weight and intention. Nothing snaps. Nothing flickers.

## What This Direction Rules Out

To stay sharp, here is what we are explicitly *not* building:
- Conventional template sections in standard order (hero → features → testimonials → contact in a vertical list)
- Bright/light theme as primary (dark canvas is non-negotiable for this direction)
- Generic stock illustrations or 3D characters
- Bouncy, springy, "playful" motion — wrong register for a software studio
- Scroll-jacking that fights the user — pinned scenes are fine; locking scroll for 8 seconds is not

## What Success Looks Like

A first-time visitor:
- Within 3 seconds feels: "this is unusual, this is intentional, this is expensive-looking."
- Within 15 seconds understands: "this studio builds software, and they treat it as craft."
- Within 60 seconds has scrolled through the homepage and remembers one specific scene (the floating glass cards assembling into the logo, the process scene, the phone mockup moment — whichever lands hardest).
- Tells someone about the site unprompted.

If we hit "tells someone about it unprompted," we've done it.
